What is "OER"?

  • Part of the Venn diagram of "free stuff on the Internet"
    • (Though OER aren't always "free" and aren't always on the Internet"
    • Often treated as a method for controlling textbook costs.
  • A philosophy about how learning objects are shared on the Internet.
  • A way of thinking about your own work and the work produced in your classrooms.

Creative Commons: A Tool For Authorial Intent

  • Copyright limits what can be done with a work. Fair use provides some limited flexibility.
  • Works in the "Public Domain" have no copyright or an expired copyright. There are no restrictions on these works.

Creative Commons: A Tool For Authorial Intent

  • Creative Commons Licensing allows authors:
    • To determine how their work is used
    • To control "degrees of openness"
    • To allow others to know how they can use a work
    • To have a ready-to-use, widely accepted license agreement
    • Allow creators to reserve some rights.

Creative Commons Allows Consumers To Freely*...

  • Use a CC-licensed work
  • Repurpose the work
  • Remix/modify the work
  • Share the work

*Within the limits specified by the author.

Creative Commons Allows Producers To...

  • Require attribution when using a CC-licensed work (BY)
  • Prevent the use of a work for commercial purposes (NC)
  • Prevent the sharing and distribution of derivative works (ND)
  • Require consumers to share derivative works under the same terms (SA)

Theory to Practice - The Open Educational Resource

5 R's of OER

The OER ecosystem thrives on the production, consumption, and redistribution of content aligned to five core principles:

  • Retain
  • Reuse
  • Revise
  • Remix
  • Redistribute

This material is an adaptation of Defining the "Open" in Open Content and Open Educational Resources, which was originally written by David Wiley and published freely under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 license at http://opencontent.org/definition/.

Retain

  • Download and control copies of the content
  • Duplicate, store, and manage copies of the content

Reuse

  • Content is made available in a usable format.
  • The format of the content can be freely modified to other formats as needed.
  • The content can be modified in ways beyond which the author originally intended.

Revise

  • Content can be altered for specific uses and needs.
  • The content can be changed and updated for new uses.

Remix

  • Content can be combined with other content to create something new.

Redistribute

  • Original content can be repackaged and reshared.
  • Newly created derivative works can be freely shared.
